    What Is Uncertainty Quantification?

    From point estimate to decision-ready uncertainty.

    Core Question

    How sure are we, and what is the cost if this estimate is wrong?

    UQ keeps uncertainty in both parameters and future outcomes, rather than collapsing everything to one number.

    \[ p(\theta \mid \mathcal{D}),\quad p(y^\ast \mid \mathcal{D}) \]

    Prior, Likelihood, Posterior (Bayes Update)

    Bayes Update

    \[ \overbrace{p(\theta\mid\mathcal{D})}^{\text{posterior}} \propto \overbrace{p(\mathcal{D}\mid\theta)}^{\text{likelihood}} \overbrace{p(\theta)}^{\text{prior}} \]

    Likelihood reweights prior belief to produce posterior belief.

    Ref: Bayesian Data Analysis (3rd ed.) — Gelman, Carlin, Stern, Dunson, Vehtari, Rubin — CRC Press — 2013.

    Explain the equation directly on-slide using overbrace labels consistently. Keep the slide focused on posterior inference only. Next we move to other uncertainty notions and their interpretation.

    Aleatoric vs Epistemic Uncertainty

    Aleatoric

    • Inherent randomness in data generation.
    • Examples: measurement noise, random contacts.
    • Not eliminated by collecting more data.

    Epistemic

    • Uncertainty from limited data/model mismatch.
    • Examples: unknown parameters, missing mechanisms.
    • Can shrink with better data or better models.

    \[ \mathrm{Var}(Z)=\mathbb{E}[\mathrm{Var}(Z\mid\Theta)] + \mathrm{Var}(\mathbb{E}[Z\mid\Theta]) \]

    \(\mathbb{E}[\mathrm{Var}(Z\mid\Theta)]\): aleatoric part \(\mathrm{Var}(\mathbb{E}[Z\mid\Theta])\): epistemic part

    Key point: more data mainly reduces epistemic uncertainty, not aleatoric noise.

    Ref: https://medium.com/data-science/

    The side-by-side comparison makes both uncertainty types visually concrete. Tell the audience that collecting more data mainly reduces epistemic uncertainty. Finally, we need to define what "trustworthy probabilities" mean: calibration.

    Three Entry Points of Uncertainty in Graph Dynamics

    Each source enters at a different location, but all three propagate to the same downstream task risk.

    Structural uncertainty \((A_{ij,t})\)

    We do not observe all true contacts; edges can be missing or misrecorded.

    \[ A_{ij,t}\sim \mathrm{Bernoulli}(p_{ij,t}) \]

    Parametric uncertainty \((\theta)\)

    Transmission or coupling strengths shift across office, dormitory, and transit settings.

    \[ \theta \sim p(\theta\mid \mathcal{D}) \]

    Observational uncertainty \((\varepsilon_t, R_t)\)

    Reports are delayed/noisy and sensing quality can drift over time.

    \[ Y_t = H_t X_t + \varepsilon_t,\quad \varepsilon_t\sim \mathcal{N}(0,R_t) \]

    The same \(Y_t\) can often be explained by multiple \((A,\theta,X)\) combinations, so dropping any one source typically yields overconfident intervals.

    All three propagate to \(p(Q\mid\mathcal{D})\)

    Ref: Discovering Latent Network Structure in Point Process Data — Linderman, Adams — ICML — 2014.

    Split uncertainty entry points into three categories to avoid labeling everything as generic noise. Each category affects the same \(Q\), but through different propagation paths. Next, we explain the core thesis: why uncertainty is not local in graphs.
